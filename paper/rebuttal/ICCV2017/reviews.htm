Paper Summary. Please summarize in your own words what the paper is about. This work presents interactive as well as automatic proofreading options to improve on typical segmentation errors produced by automatic connectomics image segmentation methods. The aim of the work is to identify potential split and merge errors in the output of an automatic segmentation and to either present such errors to a user for simple, fast correction, or to even correct automatically by using an appropriate threshold on "errorness"-probability. The proofread method is implemented as a classifier predicting split errors in presented patches. The classifier is implemented as a straight-forward CNN, trained on patches where information on patches about containing a split error is available. Inputs for the classifier are image information, borders of the automatic segmentation regions, a binary mask containing the region to be assessed if it should be split or not and a dilated border of the potential split. A simple procedure involving a dilated segmentation region and seeded-watershed from points randomly in that border enables that the same split error classifier can be used after inversion of the error probability for detecting merge errors. The proposed method is extensively evaluated on several data sets, and comparison to state of the art methods using novice and expert user interactions as well as automatic proposals are performed, showing favorable results of the novel approach.
Paper Strengths. Positive aspects of the paper. Be sure to comment on the paper's novelty, technical correctness, clarity and experimental evaluation. Notice that different papers may need different levels of evaluation: e.g., a theoretical paper vs. an application paper The paper addresses a very important research question in connectomics, since even with very good automatic segmentation methods, it is unavoidable that segmentation errors will occur. Due to the extremely large data sets involved in connectomics, these errors are still requiring a huge amount of proofreading effort. Furthermore, providing purely manual expert annotations for training pixelwise segmentation classifiers is a tedious and expensive process. The paper deals with both issues and presents an interesting and relevant novel contribution. Learning the split errors and reusing the classifier for also selecting merge error propositions is a neat idea, and - despite still showing limitations in this work - is a good starting point to build upon in future work. The methodological contribution in terms of putting the expert into the proofreading loop is relevant for being presented at ICCV. Choice of implementation of the split error classifier as a straight-forward CNN is well justified and reasonable, especially given the four input feature channels, that effectively combines all relevant information for learning the split error classifier. The paper is very well written and easy to follow. The authors provide very extensive evaluation of their work, involving user studies using a simple user interface to decide between correction options by a single click with novice and expert users as well as an automatic proposal for corrections. It is very interesting that their automated method performs very well compared to expert users. The promise to release this work to the public could be a large benefit for the research community.
